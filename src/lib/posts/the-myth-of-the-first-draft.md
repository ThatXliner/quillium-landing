"Just get the first draft done."

It's the most common piece of writing advice out there. Power through. Don't edit as you go. Get words on the page, then fix them later. The logic sounds clean: draft, revise, polish, publish.

There's just one problem. Writing doesn't work like that.

## The linear fallacy

The "first draft" model treats writing as if the hard part is execution, as if you already know what you want to say and you just need to, well, say it.

But anyone who's written anything serious knows the opposite is true. You discover what you're trying to say *by* writing. The act of putting a sentence down changes what the next sentence should be. A paragraph you write on page four retroactively invalidates your thesis on page one.

Writing is not a pipeline. It's a search through possibility space.

And yet every tool we use—Google Docs, Word, Notion, even plain text files—enforces the pipeline model.

One document. One cursor. One sequence of words from top to bottom. Want to try a different direction? Copy-paste into a new doc. Or worse, delete what you had and hope you remember it later. Or search through that "version history" hoping you'll find it before it becomes a waste of time.

The tool shapes the process. And the process shapes the output.

## Exploration vs. commitment

Here's what actually happens when experienced writers write: they hold multiple possibilities in their heads simultaneously. *This paragraph could go here, or maybe it belongs later. This argument works if I frame it this way, but there's a stronger version if I restructure the whole piece.* The good ones are constantly branching. It's just they have to do it mentally because their tools don't support it.

The "just finish the first draft" advice is really saying: stop exploring and commit to a path. And sometimes that's fine. Sometimes you need to ship. But the advice has been elevated into a philosophy of writing itself, and that's where it goes wrong.

## What if you didn't have to choose?

The real unlock isn't writing faster or forcing yourself through a linear draft. It's being able to explore multiple paths at once without losing any of them. And this is especially important for writing which requires it to be extremely well written, like college application essays or a short novel.

Imagine writing a sentence, then forking it: trying two or three different versions in parallel. Not as separate documents you have to juggle, but as branches of the same piece, visible and navigable, the way a developer might branch code.

That's what I'm building with Quillium. A writing tool where exploration is a first-class operation, not a workaround. You can fork any sentence or paragraph, follow divergent paths, and converge when you're ready—or not. Every version stays alive. Nothing gets lost to the delete key.

Writing isn't manufacturing. It's exploration. And the tools should reflect that.

## The real advice

Here's better advice than "just finish the first draft": **write more than one first draft.** Explore the possibility space. Follow the thread that surprises you, not just the first one you found.

And if your current tool makes that hard— if it forces you into a single linear sequence and punishes you for changing your mind—maybe the tool is the problem.
